Research Interests

At the center of my academic journey has been a deep curiosity to puzzle out the intricate algorithms that govern human visual perception. Back in 1999, the movie “The Matrix,” fueled me with a spark of inspiration, and this became my professional and intellectual aspiration to study Computational Neuroscience. My research goals are twofold: firstly, to investigate the computational principles and neural mechanisms in the brain that facilitate stable representations of the visual environment; and secondly, to leverage insights from Psychology and Neuroscience literature to develop a more advanced machine vision model, with the ultimate aim of delivering practical value in real-world applications.


At present, my research is focused on investigating the capacity of humans for robust visual recognition when compared to that of machines. My research has three primary areas of focus: 1) invariant object recognition, which aims to explore the mechanisms by which visual representations of objects remain invariant despite variations in appearance, and how they can be applied to out-of-distribution scenarios, 2) holistic face processing, which aims to uncover the cognitive processes underlying the holistic processing of faces and how it differs from the processing of non-face objects, and 3) recurrent computations, which aims to examine the neural recurrent dynamics of the brain in object recognition beyond traditional feedforward mechanisms.
